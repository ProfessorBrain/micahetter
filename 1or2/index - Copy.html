      About this quiz

      A simple question-based way to reflect on specialty fit.

      This is meant for first- and second-year medical students who want a rough starting point before clinical experience begins to narrow the field.

      How it works

      The app draws from a large bank of A-or-B prompts. You do not need to finish the bank; each answer adjusts the running ranking based on continuity, pace, procedures, uncertainty, population, and work setting.

      What you see

      The specialty ranking updates as you answer more prompts. Fellowship paths sharpen underneath those specialties as your pattern becomes more specific.

      What it looks at

      What this quiz is measuring

      • Patient continuity versus episodic care
      • Procedures versus longitudinal management
      • Acute decision-making versus controlled pace
      • Breadth versus narrow domain focus
      • Diagnostic intensity, teamwork, and schedule shape

      Best Use

      Close results are common. They usually suggest a broader family of specialties rather than one clearly preferred field.

      Included Fields

      Internal Medicine, Family Medicine, Pediatrics, Medicine-Pediatrics, Emergency Medicine, General Surgery, Orthopedic Surgery, Anesthesiology, Psychiatry, Radiology, Neurology, Obstetrics & Gynecology, Dermatology, Ophthalmology, Otolaryngology, Urology, Physical Medicine & Rehabilitation, Pathology, and a broad set of fellowship paths that sit within or alongside those homes.

      For reflection only. This is not career, residency, or medical advice.

      About Compare

      How to read the applicant comparison.

      This view is a descriptive reference against NRMP Charting Outcomes averages. It is meant to help you understand context, not estimate an individual probability of matching.

      Data source

      The comparison uses NRMP Charting Outcomes in the Match for U.S. MD seniors, 2024. Each specialty table reports averages among applicants who matched and applicants who did not match to their preferred specialty.

      Only specialties with a Charting Outcomes table appear in the Compare dropdown. Fellowship paths are routed to the closest available residency specialty table when a direct fellowship-specific table does not exist.

      What the radar shows

      The dashed outline represents the average matched applicant in the selected specialty. The teal shape represents the values currently entered in the controls.

      If the teal shape reaches the dashed outline on an axis, that input is at about the matched-applicant average. If it extends beyond the dashed outline, that input is above the matched-applicant average for that specialty.

      Numeric inputs

      The six numeric axes are Step 2 CK score, contiguous ranked programs, research projects, abstracts/presentations/publications, work experiences, and volunteer experiences.

      For each axis, the entered value is compared with the matched-applicant mean for that specialty. The chart is scaled so values above the mean can still be seen, but very high values are capped visually so the radar remains readable.

      Needed for 90% markers

      The Step 2 CK and contiguous-rank sliders include a small 90% marker when the NRMP probability curve crosses 0.90 for the selected specialty. These markers are estimated from the plotted Charting Outcomes probability curves, not from a new prediction model.

      If a marker is not shown for Step 2 CK, the published curve for that specialty did not reach 90% within the plotted score range. That does not mean matching is impossible or that one input controls the outcome; it only describes what that single NRMP curve displays.

      Binary individuators

      AOA, Top 40 NIH-funded medical school, PhD, and other graduate degree are combined into one radar axis called Binary Individuators.

      That axis starts at 1 rather than 0 so the chart does not visually imply that having none of those traits is alarming. Checked items add points up to a total axis range of 1 to 5.

      The added points are weighted by the specialty-specific gap between matched and unmatched applicants. A checkbox matters more when the matched percentage is meaningfully higher than the unmatched percentage in that specialty table.

      Specialty match share

      The match-share card shows the percentage of analyzed U.S. MD seniors in that specialty table who matched to their preferred specialty. It is calculated as matched applicants divided by matched plus unmatched applicants in the NRMP table.

      This is not the same thing as an individual match probability. Applicant strategy, school context, specialty-specific advising, interview performance, signaling, program list construction, geography, and application quality can all matter.

      How to use it

      Use this as a context tool: “Do my inputs look broadly similar to the average matched applicant in this specialty?” It is most useful for noticing large gaps, not for interpreting small differences.

      Meeting or exceeding every average does not guarantee a match. Falling below an average does not mean matching is impossible. Averages flatten very different applicant stories into one number.

      Important cautions

      What this does not do

      • It does not predict whether you will match.
      • It does not account for application narrative, letters, clinical grades, away rotations, interview performance, signaling strategy, program geography, or advisor input.
      • It does not know whether a research project is deeply specialty-aligned, first-author, longitudinal, or only lightly connected.
      • It does not replace specialty advising, mentorship, residency program data, or official NRMP resources.

      Best reading

      Look for broad alignment rather than precision. A profile that is close to the matched average across several axes is different from a profile that is far below on one or two major axes, but the chart should stay humble about what it can infer.

      If something looks concerning, treat it as a prompt for a conversation with an advisor rather than as a verdict.
